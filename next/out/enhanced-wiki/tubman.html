Harriet Tubman: The Conductor of Freedom

Introduction to Harriet Tubman

Harriet Tubman, born Araminta Ross in circa 1822, was an American abolitionist and political activist. Born into slavery in Maryland, Tubman escaped to freedom in the North in 1849 but returned many times to rescue both family members and non-relatives from the plantation system.

She is most famous for her role as a conductor on the Underground Railroad, a network of secret routes and safe houses used to help enslaved African Americans escape into free states and Canada. Her courageous actions helped to reshape the landscape of slavery in America.

Tubman's relentless bravery and strategic acumen not only challenged the institution of slavery but also defied the societal constraints imposed on both African Americans and women. Her daring exploits and moral conviction positioned her as one of the most significant figures in American history.

Through her actions, Tubman directly contributed to the liberation of hundreds and indirectly influenced the course of American history through her role in the abolitionist movement and the Civil War.

Early Life and Escape

Harriet Tubman was born into slavery on the Eastern Shore of Maryland, where she experienced harsh conditions and violence from a young age. In 1849, fearing a sale to the Deep South, Tubman escaped to Philadelphia, declaring, 'When I found I had crossed that line, I looked at my hands to see if I was the same person. There was such a glory over everything; the sun came like gold through the trees and over the fields, and I felt like I was in Heaven.'

This moment of liberation marked the beginning of her lifelong commitment to fighting for freedom and justice for all enslaved people.

Despite the personal risk, Tubman's initial journey to freedom was the catalyst for her transformation into one of the most formidable conductors of the Underground Railroad. Her bravery and determination were further highlighted when she decided to return to the South numerous times, risking her freedom to lead others to safety.

Her strategic use of disguises and intimate knowledge of the secretive routes of the Underground Railroad enabled her to outwit bounty hunters and navigate her charges to freedom without detection.

Role in the Underground Railroad

After her escape, Tubman risked her life to lead approximately 70 slaves to freedom over 13 missions, using the network known as the Underground Railroad. She employed clever tactics to evade capture and never lost a passenger.

Tubman's deep religious faith inspired her, and she was known as 'Moses' to many who knew her story, likening her to the biblical figure who led his people to freedom.

Her innovative tactics included traveling during winter months when nights were longer and less expected, and carrying a sedative to quiet infants. Tubman’s intelligence network was also vital in planning the timing and routes of her missions, ensuring the safety and success of the escapes.

Her leadership and bravery became legendary, making her one of the most effective and recognized 'conductors' ever to operate on the Underground Railroad. Her actions not only freed many but also struck significant blows to the institutions of slavery.

Later Life and Legacy

Beyond her work with the Underground Railroad, Tubman served as a scout, spy, guerrilla soldier, and nurse for the Union Army during the Civil War. After the war, she dedicated her life to helping impoverished former slaves and the elderly, establishing the Harriet Tubman Home for the Aged in Auburn, New York, where she later died in 1913.

Her legacy as a pioneer in civil rights and her unyielding commitment to fighting for equality and justice live on today. Tubman remains a symbol of courage and resilience, celebrated in books, movies, and by communities across the nation.

In recognition of her contributions, numerous schools, museums, and public spaces have been named in her honor, and her life and achievements are commemorated in various forms of popular culture and academia.

Tubman's life continues to inspire civil rights activism and remains a poignant story of resilience and leadership in the face of inhumanity. Her example underscores the profound impact one individual can have on the lives of many and the course of history.

Quick Facts

Harriet Tubman was born circa 1822 and died in 1913.

She led approximately 70 enslaved individuals to freedom via the Underground Railroad.
